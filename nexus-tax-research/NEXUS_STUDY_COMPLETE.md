## 1. TYPES OF NEXUS

### 1A. Physical Nexus (aka "Presence" Nexus)
Triggered by having a **physical presence** in a state:
- Office, warehouse, store, or other place of business
- Employees or independent contractors working in the state
- **Inventory stored in FBA warehouses** (critical for Amazon FBA sellers)
- Temporarily present for trade shows (varies by state — some have de minimis thresholds like 15 days)
- Drop-ship relationships in some states

**Key for you:** Amazon FBA creates physical nexus in every state where Amazon stores your inventory. You do NOT control which warehouses Amazon uses.

### 1B. Economic Nexus
Triggered by exceeding a state's **sales revenue and/or transaction thresholds** — no physical presence required. Established by *South Dakota v. Wayfair* (2018).

### 1C. Affiliate Nexus / Click-Through Nexus
Triggered by having affiliates, referral agents, or click-through advertising relationships in a state. Less relevant for pure Amazon sellers.

### 1D. Marketplace Nexus
Under **marketplace facilitator laws**, Amazon itself collects and remits sales tax on your behalf for sales made through its platform. This does NOT relieve you of registration requirements in all states or of income tax nexus.

## 3. PHYSICAL/INVENTORY NEXUS (FBA-SPECIFIC)

### 3A. How FBA Creates Physical Nexus

When you send inventory to Amazon FBA, Amazon distributes it across their warehouse network. **You have NO control over which states receive your inventory.** Wherever Amazon stores even a single unit of your product, you have established **physical nexus** in that state.

**Key points:**
- There is **no minimum quantity** of units stored — even 1 unit = nexus
- There is **no minimum duration** of storage — even 1 day = nexus
- There is **no minimum dollar value** — the mere presence of inventory triggers it
- Physical nexus applies **independently of economic nexus** thresholds
- Physical nexus creates both **sales tax** and potentially **income tax** obligations

## 4. MARKETPLACE FACILITATOR LAWS

### Critical for Amazon Sellers

As of 2026, **all 45 states with sales tax + DC + Puerto Rico** have enacted **marketplace facilitator laws**. Under these laws:

- **Amazon is required to collect and remit sales tax** on sales made through its platform
- This covers both FBA and FBM sales made through Amazon.com
- Amazon handles the calculation, collection, and remittance

### What This Means for You
- ✅ Amazon collects/remits sales tax on your Amazon platform sales in all states
- ⚠️ You may **still need to register for sales tax permits** in states where you have nexus
- ⚠️ Amazon does NOT handle **income tax** obligations — that's on you
- ⚠️ If you sell on your **own website** (non-marketplace), YOU are responsible for collecting/remitting
- ⚠️ Some states require registration even when the marketplace handles collection

### States That Require Registration Even With Marketplace Facilitator Collection
Several states still require individual sellers to register for permits even though Amazon collects. Check each state's requirements — the trend is toward requiring registration in states where you have nexus regardless.

## 5. THROWBACK & THROWOUT RULES (INCOME TAX)

### ⚠️ CRITICAL FOR CALIFORNIA-BASED BUSINESSES

Throwback and throwout rules affect **state income tax apportionment**, NOT sales tax. They determine how your business income is divided among states for income tax purposes.

### 5A. What Are Throwback Rules?

When you sell into a state where you are **not taxable** (e.g., you have no nexus there), the income from those sales gets "thrown back" to your **home state** (California) and taxed there. This means California can tax income from sales you made into other states.

### 5B. What Are Throwout Rules?

Instead of adding untaxable sales back to the numerator (throwback), throwout rules **remove** those sales from the denominator of the sales factor, effectively increasing the percentage of income attributed to the state.

### 5C. California's Rules

**California has a THROWBACK RULE for tangible property and a THROWOUT RULE for intangible property.**

This is hugely important for you because:
- If you sell tangible goods (products on Amazon) into states where you lack **income tax** nexus, that income gets **thrown back to California** and taxed at California's rate
- California's corporate tax rate is **8.84%** (C-Corp) or passes through at individual rates up to **13.3%** (LLC/S-Corp)
- The more states where you **establish income tax nexus**, the less income gets thrown back to California

**Throwout Rule (Tangible Property):** Untaxable sales removed from denominator instead.

### 5E. What This Means for a California Seller

**Example scenario:** You are based in CA, sell on Amazon nationally, and have FBA inventory in 15 states.

- States where you **have income tax nexus** (via FBA inventory): income apportioned there, taxed at that state's rate
- States where you **lack income tax nexus** (no physical presence, below income tax thresholds): income from those sales is **THROWN BACK TO CALIFORNIA** under CA's throwback rule
- **Result:** California may end up taxing a disproportionately high share of your income

**Strategy implication:** Establishing income tax nexus in more states can actually **reduce** your overall tax burden by preventing throwback to California's high tax rates — but this must be weighed against the compliance costs of filing in multiple states.

## 6. ENTITY-TYPE IMPLICATIONS: LLC vs C-Corp vs S-Corp

### 6A. Sales Tax Nexus — NO DIFFERENCE

Sales tax nexus rules apply **identically** regardless of entity type. Whether you're an LLC, C-Corp, or S-Corp:
- Same economic nexus thresholds
- Same physical nexus triggers
- Same registration requirements
- Same marketplace facilitator rules

**Sales tax is a transaction tax on the buyer, collected by the seller. Entity type is irrelevant.**

### 6B. Income Tax Nexus — SIGNIFICANT DIFFERENCES

#### LLC (Taxed as Sole Proprietorship or Partnership)

| Factor | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Federal Tax** | Pass-through: income reported on personal return (Schedule C or Form 1065/K-1) |
| **California Tax** | CA Franchise Tax Board: $800 minimum annual franchise tax + LLC fee based on gross revenue ($0-$11,790 depending on revenue tier). Income taxed at personal rates up to **13.3%** |
| **State Income Tax Filing** | Must file **personal** income tax returns in states where you have nexus. Many states require nonresident returns. |
| **Throwback Impact** | Income thrown back to CA is taxed at your **personal** California marginal rate (up to 13.3%) |
| **Self-Employment Tax** | Subject to SE tax (15.3%) on all business income if single-member |
| **P.L. 86-272 Protection** | **YES** — applies to LLCs selling tangible personal property with only solicitation activity in a state |
| **Multi-State Filing Burden** | Can be high — personal returns in every nexus state |

#### C-Corporation

| Factor | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Federal Tax** | Entity-level tax at **21%** flat rate; dividends taxed again to shareholders (double taxation) |
| **California Tax** | CA franchise tax at **8.84%** on net income (minimum $800). Plus personal tax on dividends. |
| **State Income Tax Filing** | Must file **corporate** income tax returns in states where you have nexus |
| **Throwback Impact** | Income thrown back to CA is taxed at **8.84%** corporate rate (lower than personal rates) |
| **P.L. 86-272 Protection** | **YES** — the original protection was designed for corporations |
| **Multi-State Filing Burden** | Moderate — corporate returns are generally simpler than personal multi-state filing |
| **New Hampshire** | Has a Business Profits Tax (8.5%) that applies to C-Corps — relevant if FBA inventory stored there |
| **Key Advantage** | Lower throwback rate (8.84% vs up to 13.3% personal). Also, can retain earnings at corporate level. |

#### S-Corporation

| Factor | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Federal Tax** | Pass-through: income flows to shareholders' personal returns. No entity-level federal tax. |
| **California Tax** | 1.5% entity-level tax on net income (minimum $800) + personal tax on passed-through income at rates up to 13.3% |
| **State Income Tax Filing** | Must file **both** entity-level and personal returns in many states. Some states don't recognize S-Corp status. |
| **Throwback Impact** | Income thrown back to CA is taxed at **personal** rates (up to 13.3%) plus CA's 1.5% S-Corp tax |
| **P.L. 86-272 Protection** | **YES** — applies to S-Corps selling tangible personal property |
| **Multi-State Filing Burden** | **HIGHEST** — must track state-by-state treatment of S-Corp status |
| **Key Complication** | Some states tax S-Corps at the entity level (e.g., CA's 1.5%, NH's BPT). Some states don't recognize S-Corp election. |
| **No Self-Employment Tax** | Owner can take reasonable salary + distributions (distributions not subject to SE tax) |

### 6C. States That Don't Recognize S-Corp Status (or Tax at Entity Level)

| State | S-Corp Treatment |
|-------|-----------------|
| California | 1.5% entity-level tax on net income |
| New Hampshire | Taxes S-Corps same as C-Corps (Business Profits Tax) |
| Tennessee | Historically taxed S-Corp distributions via Hall Tax (repealed 2021) |
| New York City | Does not recognize S-Corp status for city tax |
| Washington DC | Recognizes S-Corp pass-through |
| Illinois | Replacement tax of 1.5% on S-Corps |

### 6D. Comparative Tax Impact Summary (California-Based Seller)

**Scenario: $500K net income, selling into 45 states, FBA inventory in 20 states**

| Tax Component | LLC (Sole Prop) | C-Corp | S-Corp |
|--------------|----------------|--------|--------|
| Federal rate on business income | Up to 37% personal | 21% flat | Up to 37% personal |
| California rate (throwback income) | Up to 13.3% | 8.84% | Up to 13.3% + 1.5% |
| Self-employment tax | 15.3% (on first ~$160K+) | None (salary subject to FICA) | None on distributions |
| Double taxation risk | No | Yes (dividends) | No |
| Multi-state filing complexity | Moderate | Moderate | High |
| Throwback cost (CA) | Highest | Lowest | High |
| P.L. 86-272 protection | Yes | Yes | Yes |

## 7. FBA vs FBM NEXUS COMPARISON

| Factor | FBA (Fulfilled by Amazon) | FBM (Fulfilled by Merchant) |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Physical Nexus** | Created in 27+ states where Amazon stores inventory | Only in your home state (CA) unless you use 3PL warehouses |
| **Economic Nexus** | Same thresholds apply | Same thresholds apply |
| **Sales Tax Collection** | Amazon collects under marketplace facilitator laws | Amazon still collects for Amazon.com sales; YOU collect for own-website sales |
| **Income Tax Nexus** | Created in 27+ states via inventory | Only in states where you meet economic nexus thresholds for income tax |
| **Throwback Impact** | LOWER — income nexus in more states means less thrown back to CA | HIGHER — income nexus in fewer states means more thrown back to CA |
| **Compliance Burden** | Very high — potentially 27+ state income tax filings | Lower — primarily CA + states where economic nexus met |
| **Risk Profile** | Higher — nexus in states you may not even know about | Lower — more predictable |
| **Control** | LOW — Amazon decides where to ship your inventory | HIGH — you control fulfillment |

### Hybrid FBA/FBM Strategy Considerations
Since you do both FBA and FBM:
1. Your FBA inventory creates physical nexus regardless of your FBM sales
2. Your FBM sales from California only create nexus in CA (for physical) and any states where economic thresholds are met
3. Track FBA and FBM sales separately by state for accurate nexus analysis
4. Consider whether the FBA-created nexus states also have income tax obligations

## 9. ACTION ITEMS FOR CALIFORNIA-BASED SELLERS

### Immediate Steps

1. **Download your Amazon Inventory Event Detail report** to identify all states where FBA stores your inventory
2. **Register for sales tax permits** in states where you have nexus (physical or economic) — even though Amazon collects, many states require registration
3. **Assess income tax obligations** in every state where you have physical nexus via FBA inventory
4. **Evaluate entity structure** — given California's throwback rule, a C-Corp may result in lower throwback costs (8.84% vs up to 13.3%)

### Ongoing Compliance

5. **Monitor FBA warehouse changes** — Amazon regularly adds/closes fulfillment centers; your nexus footprint changes
6. **Track sales by state** monthly to identify when economic nexus thresholds are approached
7. **File income tax returns** in all nexus states (or file composite returns where available)
8. **Consider voluntary disclosure agreements (VDAs)** if you have past-due obligations in states where you had nexus but weren't filing

### Strategic Considerations

9. **Throwback mitigation:** Filing income tax returns in more states can reduce CA throwback, but weigh this against compliance costs
10. **Entity selection:** Review with your CPA whether your current entity type is optimal given multi-state obligations
11. **FBA vs FBM mix:** Consider shifting products to FBM to reduce physical nexus footprint — but evaluate whether the lost Amazon logistics benefits outweigh the tax savings
12. **Consider inventory placement options:** Amazon's FBA Inventory Placement Service can concentrate inventory in fewer locations (for a fee), potentially reducing your nexus footprint
